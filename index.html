About Me

Hello, I’m Zhihan Jiang. I'm currently a Ph.D. student from the Department of Electrical and Electronic Engineering at the University of Hong Kong, under the supervision of Prof. Edith C.H. NGAI. I received a master degree in Computer Science and Technology from Xiamen University under the supervision of Prof. Longbiao CHEN in 2021, and a bachelor degree in Computer Science and Technology from Xiamen University in 2018. My research experience spans a range of different topics in Spatiotemporal Data Analytics & Visualization, Urban Computing, Ubiquitous Computing, and Crowdsensing and Computing, such as inferring, understanding, and predicting urban traffic violation hotspots using spatial crowdsensing big data, optimizing fog radio access networks using user mobility and traffic data, re-balancing bike stations based on city-wide bike demand prediction, etc.

Research Projects

  1. Inferring, Understanding, and Predicting Urban Traffic Violation Hotspots Based on Spatial Crowdsensing Big Data
    • Traffic violations have become one of the major threats to urban transportation systems, undermining human safety and causing economic loss. Based on crowd-sensed, large scale vehicle trajectories and road environment data, we first propose a low-cost, dynamic, comprehensive framework to infer traffic violation-prone locations in cities, and then we propose to understand traffic violation hotspots leveraging point cloud-based driving simulation. Finally, we propose a three-phase framework to accurately predict traffic violation hotspots and schedule patrol tasks accordingly.
    • In the traffic violation-prone location inference framework, we first normalize the GPS trajectories with the HMM-based map matching method and extract the driving behaviors. Second, we model driver perspectives based on regression to augment the spatiotemporal contexts of driving behaviors. Third, we extract three types of traffic restrictions from those contexts, i.e., no-parking, no-turning, and speed restrictions. After matching those restrictions with driving behaviors, we get the traffic violation distribution and extract the spatiotemporal patterns of traffic violations to sense traffic violation hotspots. Finally, we build a visualization system to showcase the sensed traffic violation-prone locations.
    • In traffic violation hotspot understanding framework, we first model the traffic violation-prone scenes based on point clouds and temporal contexts to accurately restore drivers’ perspectives at the scenes. Second, we leverage a hybrid approach of inductive and deductive analysis to derive indicators of drivers’ visual and cognitive loads in traffic violation hotspots based on the literature and drivers’ feedback from experiencing driving at the scenes in a VR driving simulator. Third, we build an explainable diagnosing model based on the derived indicators to estimate loads in other crowd-sensed traffic scenes automatically and systematically.
    • In traffic violation hotspot prediction and patrol task scheduling framework, we first cluster scattered traffic violations into traffic violation hotspot candidates with a proposed road network-based clustering algorithm (RCA). Then we leverage cross-domain heterogeneous environment data to predict traffic violation hotspots with a proposed context-aware self-adaptive learning model (CSTA). In the patrol task allocation phase, we propose a tensor-based integer linear problem modeling method (TILP) to find optimal vertex-disjoint patrol routes with maximal coverage of traffic violation hotspots under resource constraints.
    • Demos: [Traffic Violation Visualization] [Traffic Sign Visualization][VR Driving Simulator][Patrol Route Visualization]
  2. Understanding Urban Structures and Crowd Dynamics Leveraging Large-Scale Vehicle Mobility Data
    • To facilitate efficient urban planning and effective city management, we propose a two-phase framework to analyze urban structures and crowd dynamics leveraging vehicle mobility data. In the urban structure portrait phase, we divide the city into fine-grained mobility grids and propose a distance-constrained clustering algorithm (DCCA) to cluster the grids with similar mobility features into structured urban areas. In the crowd dynamic characterization phase, we detect irregular mobility traffic patterns in each area leveraging an ARIMA-based anomaly detection algorithm (ADAM), and then correlate them to the urban social and emergency events extracted from social media.
    • Demo: [Urban Structure and Crowd Dynamic Visualization]
  3. Optimizing Fog Radio Access Networks for 5G Leveraging User Mobility and Traffic Data
    • To optimize the fog-computing-based radio access network (Fog-RAN), we proposed a two-phase data-driven framework to cluster base stations (RRHs) into communities and find optimal data processing unit (BBU) allocation schemes that maximize BBU utilization rate. In the RRH clustering phase, we build a weighted graph model to characterize user mobility patterns across RRHs and propose a size-constrained community detection (SCUD) algorithm to cluster RRHs into communities with frequent internal handover events. In the BBU allocation phase, we formulate BBU allocation in each community fog server as a set partitioning problem and propose a column-reduced integer programming (CLIP) algorithm to find optimal BBU allocation schemes that maximize BBU utilization rate.
    • Demo: [ Fog-RAN Optimization Visualization]
  4. Deep Bike Demand Forecast and Crowd-Driven Station Re-balancing for Bike Sharing Systems
    • Bike sharing systems are widely operated in many cities as a green transportation means to solve the last mile problem and to reduce traffic congestion. In this work, we propose a crowd-driven station re-balancing mechanism to accurately forecast city-wide bike demands and effectively re-balance bike stations. First, we represent the bike demand trend pattern as the slope and duration to accurately forecast over-demand stations at the station-level. Then we propose a spatiotemporal graph neural networks (ST-GNN) to model and forecast station-level bike demands trends. Second, we generate candidate station re-balancing tasks by formulating the task generation problem as a transportation problem under the lowest cycling distance objective. Finally, we select potential commuting participants from existing cyclists and Uber riders and propose an incentive mechanism to effectively allocate station re-balancing tasks to these users by sending them red packets.
